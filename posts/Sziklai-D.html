NOTES

  • George Sziklai, a Hungarian born in 1909, invented a configuration of standard BJT transistors. This configuration was named the Sziklai pair, although you might see it referred to as the complmentary feedback pair in modern days.
  • The Sziklai Advantage
    • Negative feedback is included inherently within the transistor design, protecting from thermal runaway, and reducing distortion.
  • Powered from a single-supply: +9v DC all the way to the component's maximum (consult datasheet). Be mindful of the electrolytic voltage values!
  • The feedback values in this circuit are fairly fine-tuned for my phone's measured audio jack output which was 0.11v RMS, or 0.31v Peak-to-Peak. I chose these values by playing around in LTSpice (a circuit simulation software) and comparing the distortion on the output until it was within my acceptable levels. At +9v DC, I can crank the volume and don't run into (audible) distortion. At +8v DC, from a car battery under load, it clips at the highest two volume-notches, which is good enough for my use-case.
  • I also discovered it to sound amazing with a guitar as an input, but I would recommend larger output transistors with more power dissipation, or at least a heat-sink/fan, as guitar's can have voltage transients of up to 5v!
  • The op-amp circuit is louder, but with +9v DC single-supply it's pretty rough on the ol' distortion. With a ±9v or larger split-power supply, this would be much more viable and offer more gain with less distortion, overall.
  • It is best practice to add small-value (0R22), high-wattage resistors (5W or more) on the output "emitters" (collectors of Q2, Q4) of the output-stage transistors. This increases linearity for the output stage at different temperatures (although the Sziklai pair is already very linear to begin with), and, most importantly, limits current as temperature rises. This further prevents thermal runaway, although I found it to be stable with TO-92 output transistors and no heat-sink.
