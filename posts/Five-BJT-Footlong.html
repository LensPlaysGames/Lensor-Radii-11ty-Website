FACTUAL NOTES

  • Low Input Impedance = Low Amplification

  • Bootstrapping, or positive feedback, is the action of taking an output from an amplifier and feeding it back into the input. This increases the input impedance, which inherently increases the amplification (read: gain)

  • Negative feedback is the action of taking an out of phase output from an amplifier and feeding it back into the input

    • The Good

      • Reduced noise
      • Reduced distortion
      • Widened amplifier bandwidth (frequency range)

    • Da Baad

      • Negative feedback can become positive feedback due to stray capacitance and inductance from manufacturing or environmental changes (as well as trace inductance, trace capacitance, actual inductors or actual capacitors) shifting the phase of the NFB signal

ACTUAL ARTICLE

     If you need a refresher on transistors and how they work, here is a great resource I found.

     In this article, I explain both the function and how to build a common-collector Class AB power amplifier with a common-emitter Class A driver, as well as negative feedback and bootstrapping. I lovingly named this amplifier the 'Five BJT Footlong', due to my PCB design having them all at the top, in a row. I designed the circuit with darlington transistors, as this increases the input impedace (a la gain) with minimal repercussions for (my) audio applications. This also takes some thermal strain off of each individual transistor, allowing me to use tiny TO-92 package transistors, which are just 4.58mm in width and height, plus the leads.

     This circuit can be powered anywhere from around +9v DC to (technically) the max that your transistors allow, although I don't recommend that unless you have a very serious heat-sink attached very well to your output stage and biasing components. I tried +5v DC from a USB cable, but I found that it is almost impossible to get the bias correct in a way that it does not oscillate, or clip immediately due to only having around ±1v of headroom (if that) at the output. PATHETIC!

     The input signal can be viewed as 'starting out' flowing through R1. This increases the input impedance of our driver to increase gain, and in case of low-output impedance devices used as an input.

     C1 is up next, this is a coupling capacitor, which removes all DC components of the electronic signal and lets the AC flow (mostly) unharmed. If the value of C1 is too small, you will notice a decreased bass response. If too large, you may notice the transients are not as sharp as before, although this can be hard to hear audibly at reasonable capacitor levels.

     Q1, an NPN transistor, acts as the driver (voltage amplifier), making sure the output stage has enough current and voltage, not just relying on whatever input you happen to plug in to supply your valuable, valuable electrons. This circuit could also be driven by an op-amp, like the NE5532 or similar, but a simple class A transistor amplifier works just as well. R4, an emitter resistor, is used to stabilise the performance of the driver circuit, albeit at the cost of less gain, overall.

     Q2 and Q3, both NPN transistors, as well as Q4 and Q5, both PNP transistors, make up the output-stage. There are two of each because they form darlington transistor pairs. As these are used within a current amplification stage, they have a tendency to get hot if driving them loud. You could also build this exact circuit without the darlington pairs (as I did, at first), but, like me, you'll come to realize those transistors get so hot they boil water off of your fingers (with no heatsink, in a TO-92 package). So, yeah, that's something I can't recommend unless you have a sufficient heat-sink and/or a different transistor rated for higher current.

     If you haven't learned about darlington transistors yet, check out this great article on them by Electronics Tutorials.

Biasing transistors can be hard to understand, but stay with me.

     Now, what are all those diodes in a row for? I'll explain. BJT, or Bipolar Junction Transistors, need a certain voltage at the base before it will start conducting (Usually around 0.6v to 0.7v (600-700mV)). This means if the transistors are not correctly biased, any input signal will lose, or be clipped below that 'turn-on' voltage. The effect this has on the signal is called crossover distortion.

     To minimise this crossover distortion, the transistor(s) can be 'pre-supplied' with the voltage it needs to start conducting, meaning it will always be amplifying when the input signal (big or small) hits the base of the transistor. Since diodes have a similar constant voltage drop to BJT transistors (suspiciously the same), those can be used to get the voltages needed for proper biasing of our output-stage transistors. Because there are four base-emitter junctions within the path that the signal takes through this amplification stage (two for each darlington pair), 4 x 0.7v (2.8v) of biasing is needed, AKA four diodes worth. This also means that the output voltage swing will be offset by this amount from the power rails. That explains why +5v DC does not work great to power this particular circuit, as the output voltage swing was limited to ±1.1v (0.78v RMS). Pop those four diodes in series between the bases of the darlingtons and OOH baby she roars (math coming later). To put it more absolutely, the entire input signal can now pass through the amplifier without the quiet bits being oh-so-violently ripped from the beautiful soundscape it once was.

     The diodes can also be replaced with a transistor and a few resistors, which are more easily mounted to a heat-sink, allowing for thermal-runaway protection to be easily implemented (more on this below).

     C2 is a coupling capacitor for the output, preventing any DC from getting to your speaker (or whatever load you are using). It also provides instant current to the speaker if it happens to need it (for example, during a heavy bass hit). As the value of this capacitor increases, more bass, or low-end, can be provided by the amp (to an extent). Now, I'm a bass-head, so I put the largest cap I had on hand in there (4700uF).

     C3 is VERY IMPORTANT. It's value should be as large as possible, really. If left out, the whole thing can oscillate (make sound with no input), and the output is very, very distorted (almost sounds like a growl). It may seem useless to put a capacitor between power and ground, but it makes all the difference.

     It is best practice to add a small-value resistor in-between the output and the emitters of the output stage transistors (in between Q3 and C2, as well as Q5 and C2). This (as resistors do) attenuates the output (making it quieter), but gives some very nice-to-have features. The most prominent of those being that the circuit no longer relies on the transistor itself to set gain. That means all that horrible non-linearity that transistors spit out due to temperature, humidity, and more environmental/manufacturing variables is removed (reduced) and now the circuit relies on a nice, linear resistor to set it's gain. These would serve the same purpose for the output stage as R4 does for the driver stage.

     Also best practice: any components used to bias the output stage (D1 through D4, in this case) should be mounted to the same heatsink as the output stage transistors. If this is not done, thermal runaway can commence. Thermal runaway is something that happens when a variable increases the device's temperature, increasing the variable, which increases the device's temperature, and so on. For our output stage transistors, this variable is the amount of current flowing from collector to emitter. As that current increases, the transistors dissipate more power, increasing their temperature. A hotter transistor allows more current to flow from collector to emitter, starting the whole process over again. Obviously, we don't want our transistors to keep getting hotter and hotter and hotter as they operate, so what gives? Well, diodes have what can be thought of as the reverse temperature response of transistors. As diodes heat up, the current they allow to flow decreases. This means if the diodes share a thermal mass with the output transistors, everything can balance out and keep each other in check. The diodes resist current if things get too hot, and the transistors amplify current if things are cold enough. If you are familiar with diode design, you'll know that the packages they come in usually are not best for mounting on a heat-sink, which is why an easy-to-mount transistor can be used to replace these diodes (Diagram below).

FEEDBACK

     R2, connected to the shared emitters of the output stage, routes a portion of the output signal back to the input, which is now out-of-phase, of the output-stage transistors. Mixing a signal with an out-of-phase one will reduce the overall amplitude, so what's going on here? Well, it does reduce the overall gain of the circuit. There's no getting around that we are reducing amplitude, here. It also has a tendency to cause oscillation if improper values are chosen. So, why even include it if it reduces amplification and causes problems? Well, it also gives us a range of benefits that are very useful, even in audio applications. One of those is reliability, or really, reproducibility. Transistor's functional values, like the amount of gain they produce, are very dependant on variable factors, like temperature, and humidity, even. This means that in a cold room, you could blow out your speakers, and in a toasty sauna, it might not even amplify the input at all. For commercial products, this is not good. To get around this environmental dependancy, we can set the gain of the amplifier using resistors, which have a linear response to many variables, including temperature. This gives us a solid, non-variable gain amount that is easily used in calculations, and no temperature effects have to be taken into account. Hurray! Now we can enjoy our tunes in the hot of the summer, and also in the freezing cold of the winter, with minimal variation. That's what I call reliable.

     R3, also connected to the shared emitters of the output stage (just after a coupling capacitor, C2), routes a portion of the output signal back to the input. You may recognize this as the definition for positive feedback, or bootstrapping. This is the easiest type of feedback to understand, as you take an amplified signal, 'turn it down' through a resistor, and then feed it back into the input to give a small boost back to the output.

    You may be wondering what the wattage of this simple, cheap amplifier is. That means it's time for: MATH!

MATH

     Ohm's law tells us that voltage (V), current (I), resistance (Ω), and power (W) are all related mathematically. Using Ohm's law, we can use any two of those numbers (for example: voltage and current), and solve for the other two (resistance and power). This means if we know any of those two things about the output signal, then we could solve for our power, AKA our wattage! Now, I'm poor, and I haven't been able to afford a multimeter, but I do have LTSpice (a free circuit simulation software) and some determination. After building up the circuit and running the ".tran 100ms" command, I was greeted with a smooth sine-wave on the output, meaning distortion was relatively low! Hurray! The input peak-to-peak voltage is 2v, with a current of 200µA. The output voltage swing (peak-to-peak voltage) is around 7.5v, while the current sat right above at 1A. Luckily, P = IV, (you could also use P = V²/R successfully). The input wattage works out to be 400µW, which is very small. To calculate the output wattage of our Five-BJT (not-so) Footlong amplifier, we just multiply those two output measurements together! One of them being 1, that makes this very easy, giving us (~7.5v * ~1A) = ~7.5W. That means the power gain of the amplifier works out to (roughly) 18750. That's a lot of damage. Not bad for five transistors, 'low' (hardly audible) distortion, minimal heat, and some forethought.

     Taking a look at the FFT (a way to visualize audio signals, mainly distortion (image below)), I found that the second-order harmonic of the output signal was down at -30dB, while the fundamental sat atop it's pedestal at +9dB. With a 39dB difference, that calculates out to a 1.1% second-order harmonic. Looking at the rest of the FFT (within the audio range, which can be deceiving on the LTSpice FFT), I can see that, most-likely, THD (Total Harmonic Distortion) is under 10% with a 2v peak-to-peak input (which is fairly high for, let's say, the headphone out on a phone or similar device). While that THD is not great in the hi-fi world (or any world, really), that is good enough for casual listening, and most people cannot hear the distortion until presented with a clean tone as well.
