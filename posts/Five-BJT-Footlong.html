FACTUAL NOTES

  • Low Input Impedance = Low Amplification

  • Bootstrapping, or positive feedback, is the action of taking an output from an amplifier and feeding it back into the input. This increases the input impedance, which inherently increases the amplification (read: gain)

  • Negative feedback is the action of taking an out of phase output from an amplifier and feeding it back into the input

    • The Good

      • Reduced noise
      • Reduced distortion
      • Widened amplifier bandwidth (frequency range)

    • Da Baad

      • Negative feedback can become positive feedback due to stray capacitance and inductance from manufacturing or environmental changes (as well as trace inductance, trace capacitance, actual inductors or actual capacitors) shifting the phase of the NFB signal

ACTUAL ARTICLE

     If you need a refresher on transistors and how they work, here is a great resource I found.

     In this article, I explain both the function and how to build a Class AB power amplifier with Class A driver, negative feedback, and bootstrapping.

     This circuit can be powered anywhere from +9v DC to (technically) the max that your transistors allow, although I don't recommend that unless you have a very serious heat-sink attached very well to your output stage. I tried +5v DC from a USB cable, but I found that it is almost impossible to get the bias correct in a way that it does not oscillate, or clip immediately due to only having around ±1v of headroom.

     The input signal starts out flowing through R1, which just increases the input impedance of our driver in case of low-output impedance devices used as an input.

     C1 is up next, this is a coupling capacitor, which removes all DC components of the electronic signal and lets the AC flow (mostly) unharmed. If the value of C1 is too small, you may notice a decreased bass response. If too large, you may notice the transients are not as sharp as before, although this can be hard to hear audibly at first.

     Q1, an NPN transistor, acts as the driver, making sure the output stage has enough current and power, not just relying on whatever input you happen to plug in to supply your valuable, valuable electrons. This circuit could also be driven by an op-amp, like the NE5532 or similar, but a simple transistor works just as well when biased correctly.

     Q2 and Q3, both NPN transistors, as well as Q4 and Q5, both PNP transistors, make up the output-stage. There are two of each because they form darlington transistor pairs. You could also build this exact circuit without the darlington pairs (as I did, at first), but, like me, you'll come to realize those transistors get so hot they boil water off of your fingers, even with VCC+ @ +12v DC and the input coming from a 3.5mm jack on a phone. So, yeah, that's something I can't recommend unless you have a sufficient heat-sink and/or a transistor rated for high-current.

     If you haven't learned about darlington transistors yet, check out this great article on them by Electronics Tutorials.

Biasing transistors can be hard to understand, but stay with me.

     Now, what are all those diodes in a row for? I'll explain. BJT, or Bipolar Junction Transistors, need a certain voltage at the base before it will start conducting (Usually around 0.6v to 0.7v). This means if the transistors are not correctly biased, any input signal will lose, or be clipped below that certain voltage. This is called crossover distortion.

     To minimise this crossover distortion, the transistor(s) can be 'pre-supplied' with the voltage it needs to start conducting, meaning it will always be amplifying when the input signal (big or small) hits the base of the transistor. Since diodes have a similar constant voltage drop to BJT transistors (suspiciously the same), those can be used to get the voltages needed for proper biasing of our output-stage transistors. Because there are four base-emitter junctions within the path that the signal takes through this amplification stage (two for each darlington pair), 4 x 0.7v (2.8v) of biasing is needed, AKA four diodes worth. This also means that the output voltage swing will be offset by this amount from the power rails. That explains why +5v DC does not work great to power this particular circuit, as the output voltage swing was limited to ±2.2v (0.78v RMS). Pop those four diodes in series between the bases of the darlingtons and OOH baby she roars (math coming later). To put it more absolutely, the entire input signal can now pass through the amplifier without the quiet bits being oh-so-violently ripped from the beautiful soundscape it once was.

     C2 is a coupling capacitor for the output, preventing any DC from getting to your speaker (or whatever load you are using). It also provides instant current to the speaker if it happens to need it (for example, during a heavy bass hit).

     C3 is VERY IMPORTANT. If left out, the whole thing can oscillate (make sound with no input), and the output is very, very distorted (almost sounds like a growl). It may seem useless to put a capacitor between power and ground, but it makes all the difference.

     It is best practice to add a small-value resistor in-between the output and the emitters of the output stage transistors. This (as resistors do) attenuates the output (making it quieter), but gives some very nice-to-have features. The most prominent of those being that the circuit no longer relies on the transistor itself to set gain. That means all that horrible non-linearity that transistors spit out due to temperature, humidity, and more environmental/manufacturing variables is removed (reduced) and now the circuit relies on a nice, linear resistor to set it's gain.

     Also best practice: any components used to bias the output stage (D1 through D4, in this case) should be mounted to the same heatsink as the output stage transistors. If this is not done, thermal runaway can commence. Thermal runaway is something that happens when a variable increases the device's temperature, increasing the variable, which increases the device's temperature, and so on. For our output stage transistors, this variable is the amount of current flowing from collector to emitter. As that current increases, the transistors dissipate more power, increasing their temperature. A hotter transistor allows more current to flow from collector to emitter, starting the whole process over again. Obviously, we don't want our transistors to keep getting hotter and hotter and hotter as they operate, so what gives? Well, diodes have what can be thought of as the reverse temperature response of transistors. As diodes heat up, the current they allow to flow decreases. This means if the diodes share a thermal mass with the output transistors, everything can balance out and keep each other in check. The diodes resist current if things get too hot, and the transistors amplify current if things are cold enough. If you are familiar with diode design, you'll know that the packages they come in usually are not best for mounting on a heat-sink, which is why an easy-to-mount transistor can be used to replace these diodes (Diagram below).

FEEDBACK

     R2, connected to the shared emitters of the output stage, routes a portion of the in-phase output signal back to the out-of-phase input of the output-stage transistors. Mixing an in-phase signal with an out-of-phase one will reduce the overall amplitude, so what's going on here? Well, it does reduce the overall gain of the circuit. There's no getting around that we are reducing amplitude, here. It also has a tendency to cause oscillation if improper values are chosen. So, why even include it if it reduces amplification and causes problems? Well, it also gives us a range of benefits that are very useful, even in audio applications. One of those is reliability, or really, reproducibility. Transistor's functional values, like the amount of gain they produce, is very dependant on variable factors, like temperature, and humidity, even. This means that in a cold room, you could blow out your speakers, and in a toasty sauna, it might not even amplify the input at all. For commercial products, this is not good. To get around this environmental dependancy, we can set the gain of the amplifier using resistors, which have a linear temperature response. This gives us a solid, non-variable gain amount that is easily used in calculations, and no temperature effects have to be taken into account. Hurray! Now we can enjoy our tunes in the hot of the summer, and also in the freezing cold of the winter, with minimal variation. That's what I call reliable.

     R3, also connected to the shared emitters of the output stage (just after a coupling capacitor, C2), routes a portion of the in-phase output signal back to the (now) out-of-phase input. You may recognize this as the definition for positive feedback, or bootstrapping. This is the easiest type of feedback to understand, as you take an amplified signal, 'turn it down' through a resistor, and then feed it back into the input to give a small boost back to the output.

    You may be wondering what the wattage of this simple, cheap amplifier is. That means it's time for: MATH!

MATH

     Ohm's law tells us that voltage (V), current (I), resistance (Ω), and power (W) are all related mathematically. Using Ohm's law, we can use any two of those numbers (for example: current and resistance), and solve for the other two (voltage and power). This means if we know the output load, or the resistance of the speaker used (common values are 4, 8, and 16Ω), as well as the current that flows at the output stage, then we could solve for our power, AKA our wattage! Now, I'm poor, and I haven't been able to afford a multimeter, but I do have the datasheets for the components I'm using. For the 2N5551 and 2N5401 transistors, both have a maximum collector-emitter current of 600mA. Because the output is connected within this collector-emitter path, it is limited to this current. I tested on a variety of speakers, but the one heard in the sound test has a value of 8Ω. Ohm's law tells us that with current and resistance, we are able to solve for voltage and power, which, in this case, is the max output voltage swing and max output wattage.

     After a little math, I found this circuit to have a (theoretical) max output power of 2.88W, and a max output voltage swing of ±4.8v (1.7v RMS) with an 8Ω load. At 16Ω, this puppy can deliver over 5 Watts. This will blow all those tiny speakers you have laying around, and then some! Not bad for five transistors and a few passive components, if I do say so myself.
