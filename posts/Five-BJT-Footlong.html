FACTUAL NOTES

  • Low Input Impedance = Low Amplification

  • Bootstrapping, or positive feedback, is the action of taking an output from an amplifier and feeding it back into the input. This increases the input impedance, which inherently increases the amplification (read: gain)

  • Negative feedback is the action of taking an out of phase output from an amplifier and feeding it back into the input

    • The Good

      • Reduced noise
      • Reduced distortion
      • Widened amplifier bandwidth (frequency range)

    • Da Baad

      • Negative feedback can become positive feedback due to stray capacitance and inductance from manufacturing or environmental changes (as well as trace inductance, trace capacitance, actual inductors or actual capacitors) shifting the phase of the NFB signal

ACTUAL ARTICLE

If you need a refresher on transistors and how they work, now is a great time to do so.

    Q1 acts as the driver, making sure the output stage has enough current and power, not just relying on whatever input you happen to plug in to supply your valuable, valuable electrons.

Q2 and Q3, as well as Q4 and Q5 make up darlington transistor pairs. If you haven't learned about those yet, check out this great article on them by Electronics Tutorials.

Biasing transistors can be hard to understand, but stay with me.

    BJT, or Bipolar Junction Transistors, need a certain voltage at the base before it will start conducting (Usually around 0.7v, or 700mV). This means if the transistors are not biased, any signal amplified will lose, or be clipped below that certain voltage. This is called crossover distortion.

     To overcome this, the transistor can be 'pre-supplied' with 0.7v, meaning it will always be conducting, therefore amplifying, when our signal (big or small) hits the base. Since diodes have a similar constant voltage drop to BJT transistors (suspiciously the same), we can use those to get the voltages we need for the biasing. Because there are four base-emitter junctions within the path that the signal takes through this amplification stage (two for each darlington pair), we need 4 x 0.7v of biasing, AKA four diodes worth. Pop those in series between the bases of the darlingtons and OOH baby she roars. To put it more absolutely, the entire signal can now pass through without the quiet bits being oh-so-violently ripped from the beautiful soundscape it once was.
